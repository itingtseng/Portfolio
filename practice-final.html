Interactive Design Practice

We design a solution for improving citizen engagement through design process including problem framing, design exploration, brainstorming, prototyping, and evaluating.

OVERVIEW

About the project

Politics and their decisions directly affect our lives. Government dysfunction continues to surpass the economy as one of the most serious problems. According to a survey six out of ten Americans rate the economy as weak. Partly in response to this growing disaffection, a wave of participatory policy reform has emerged in America’s largest cities, capitalizing on new technology and democratic experiments that aim to improve democracy.

GOAL

We focused our solution on tackling the issue of ‘lack of civic education’ and we brainstormed possible solutions around the traditional civic education system for students. We came across civic engagement as a lens to our solution as it was more of an active participation where students can experience real public action in contrast to the traditional lecture system. Civic engagement is a practical approach to providing civic knowledge as well as understand the wicked problems of democracy that may arise when trying to solve them.

PROBLEM FRAMING

Civic education and public engagement are at an all-time low in the United States. A 2016 survey by the Annenberg Public Policy Center found that only 26 percent of Americans can name all three branches of government. Not surprisingly, public trust in government is at only 18 percent and voter participation is also very low.

Lack of civic education has made citizens more susceptible to political advertising, which has given the wealthy tremendous power to shape politics through campaign contributions and ad spending. So few Americans trust the political system that nearly half of 2016 primary votes went to candidates promising anti-establishment revolutions. Especially students are coming into higher education without the background to even know and understand competing ideas and without the ability to discuss and debate on them.

Without an understanding of the structure of government; rights and responsibilities; and methods of public engagement, civic literacy, and voter apathy are currently plaguing American democracy. We realized that educators and schools have a unique opportunity and responsibility to ensure that youth becomes engaged and knowledgeable citizens in order to actively participate in politics and make more informed voting decisions. Currently, civic education in the classroom has been cast to the side in most schools. Instead, the focus has shifted to promoting more “practical” courses like math and science.

IDEATION

Electronic participatory budgeting is the concept we chose when we converged upon possible solutions. This concept has lead to an increase in civic education and engagement. The main goal of our social computing product is to provide the necessary civic knowledge and improve the engagement of students. Here university students are given the power to decide on where a part of the budget should be spent at a school level. When the people most impacted by a decision are brought into the decision-making process and given control, there are better decisions made, stronger relationships built within communities, and higher levels of engagement as a result.

This concept is already in existence and we are not reinventing the wheel. This concept is implemented in 29 cities in the US and more than 400,000 people were able to directly decide how to spend $299 million in public funds. This process is currently being implemented in NYC.
We are providing this social computing product at an educational level where we are trying to make the participatory budgeting more engaging, quicker to implement, and more unbiased by leveraging the use of technology.

CHALLENGES

In our brainstorming sessions, each one had its own differences in the way that they were conducted and the results that they produced. Our first session was heavily team-driven as all three members generated ideas together. Three 10-minute segments were spent on finding 10-12 ideas on the three design exploration topics of our problem-framing report (education, visualization, and incentivizing). This session laid out the foundation of ideas that our future sessions would then build and expand upon. Our second session was done in collecting ideas from other students who brainstormed based on the same three design exploration topics. The results they produced helped give us a broader view of areas to look toward in design opportunities. Our third session was then done with emphasis on individual idea generation, where each group member came up with 10-12 ideas individually which we would then share. At this point of our brainstorming, ideas produced consisted of a mix of some new, some similar, and some added onto pre-existing topics. When choosing what ideas to use for our solution, we did not necessarily pick from a specific session. Rather, we saw all three sessions as a pool of ideas in whole, and we figured that based on each of the three design exploration topics, we tried to come up with a solution of each that integrated multiple ideas together. We also took our feedback from the previous report into consideration, thinking about how each idea would apply to a larger societal and cultural scale as opposed to an individual level, and how each solution implemented more action for sustainability beyond just persuasion.

DESIGN EXPLORATIONS

Our design direction focuses on increasing civic engagement by incorporating modern democratic practices across the campuses. We realized that deliberative engagement, Concord and public action can solve the wicked problems of democracy today and to eradicate the crisis of legitimacy in our current education system where most of the learning is focused on getting jobs and being financially independent at first. However, incorporating engaging environment across the campus can make today's youth ready for the responsible and deliberative engagement towards society in the future. With our design direction, we focused on creating a map-based system which allows the students to engage across their school campuses by instantly submitting an idea to the problem they are experiencing. These submissions go on a platform, which is a public forum and is connected with the school's database to record and process the data of the entities involved in the system. The algorithm to enact on the quick survey request takes places based on the location and the location information in the school's database. Collaborative inputs are gathered to gauge the legitimacy of the problems and idea being submitted to the forum. Based on the inputs gathered, from quick surveys along with a timely brainstorming session organized to effectively engage and develop an understanding not only between students and University delegates but also for other university staff. Once top problem pitches are developed and agreed upon, participatory budgeting will further allow users to converge upon proposed solutions for the common problems. However, after getting feedback from Lynn, we plan to not only explore more towards the learning of modern democratic practices but also explore the gap in our system for the following challenges,

1. System to prioritize the severity and scope of the problem.

2. Address and differentiate public reasoning of all races, genders, levels of population.

How to enact teenagers for deliberative engagement and make them articulate civic problems freely.

How to address the inclusivity of lower-class students who are not equipped with technology.

Recognition of community-committed students to keep them motivated for continuous engagement.

RECOMMENDATIONS

The selection process of the ideas is still unclear in the initial prototype. We need to include interactions/information on how these ideas are selected. Civic education process needs to be explained to the users through animation, visuals and effective copy. It is still unclear how we complement the offline process with the online process for the inclusivity of low-class students not equipped with technology. The app needs to provide information regarding the offline community activity/event and incorporate the results from the event into the mobile application. There are opportunities to integrate features that enhance collaborative effort such as discussion forums and webinars or third party users to evaluate or critique the ideas to reduce biased perspective. We would explore effective ways to prioritize problems based on severity and scope of the problem.
